The Tire Company That Ate the World

How a French tire manufacturer became the world's leading authority on food

By Areeba Fatima

The Michelin Guide, which often influences people's decision to try a restaurant in a new country, was originally conceived as a little guidebook for tourists, to encourage roadtrips. And like most successful marketing campaigns, the guide got a life of its own, establishing itself as the global authority on culinary success.

In 1886, a German engine designer Carl Benz developed the Benz Patent Motor Car. Someone had finally figured out how to mount an internal combustion engine to a carriage.

Three years later, in a small French town called Clermont-Ferrand, Andre Michelin and his brother Edouard Michelin founded a tyre company. At that time there were only 3,000 cars in the country.

As time passed, the brothers realized they had to innovate their marketing strategy to convince more people to get on the road. They launched the Michelin Guide, a handbook with maps, information on how to change a tire, how to fill your car up with fuel.

Two decades later, they innovated their methodology in a way that would change how the word 'Michelin' is perceived forever. They added a list of hotels and restaurants in Paris.

The guide blew up. The Michelin brothers then hired a team of 'mystery diners.'

In six years, the Michelin Guide had accumulated serious acclaim, and the team started awarding their favorite restaurants a single star. From one star to another, and then another, the guide continued to grow, both in popularity and its ranking methodology.

This is where the stars fell in 2024.

In 2024, the Michelin Guide ranked 3,589 restaurants in 47 countries around the world

France, home of the Michelin stars, maintained the lead with 629 Michelin starred restaurants.

533 French restaurants were given 1 star, 66 were given 2, and 30 were given 3 Michelin stars.
